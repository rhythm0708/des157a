Project Improvements

I designed a usability test to identify bugs or missing features that affect the user experience of my game. I selected this approach for two reasons: one, it allows me to adjust the scope based on the amount of time I have this week; and two, a game’s quality is strongly tied to user experience.

To gather expert feedback, I leveraged my role as Co-President of the Game Development & Arts Club (GDAC) to organize a Playtesting Night during Week 10. I didn’t just do this for the class: I think it is a great opportunity for students in any game or game-adjacent class to get feedback on their work before final submissions. But I digress.

Before I attend the event, it is crucial that I fix all major playability issues outlined in my design brief. This ensures that the feedback I receive during playtesting is useful and non-redundant, i.e. they highlight new and thoughtful challenges rather than reiterating problems that I am already aware of and am actively working to fix.

I plan to conduct playtesting with at least three participants during the event. I aim to adhere to standard blind playtesting protocol:

  1. Allow participants to playtest the game without any help or interruption.
  2. Record the time it takes for each participant to finish (or DNF, if applicable).
  3. After they finish, ask the following questions:
    1. What are your overall thoughts on the experience? Did you enjoy the mystery?
    2. How would you grade the difficulty of the game? Do you think it can be completed within an appropriate timeframe?
    3. Were there any UI elements that you found prohibitive?
    4. Are there any additional features you’d like to see in a game like this?

The number of questions is intentionally limited to respect participants’ time (also because I anticipate the quality of responses will degrade past a certain number). I focus more on general and actionable items that have a direct impact on play.
